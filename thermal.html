Thermal Control

Managing extreme temperatures in the space environment

Key Facts

Space does not have an intrinsic temperature where no particles exist; temperature is a property of matter and radiation interaction.

The Universe's background temperature is ~2.7 K (CMB). The coldest astrophysical object measured: Boomerang Nebula ~1 K.

Spacecraft and habitats see extremes: sunlight-facing surfaces can exceed +100 °C, shaded surfaces can drop below −170 °C. Thermal control is essential.

Thermal Control Systems

Passive: Multi-layer insulation (MLI), thermal coatings and paints, heat pipes, thermal straps, phase change materials (PCMs), sunshields and louvers, deployable radiators.

Active: Pumped fluid loops (internal water loops), mechanically pumped loops, thermoelectric coolers (TECs), thermal switches.

Balance Equation: Q_gen (crew + electronics + solar gain) = Q_out,rad (emitted via radiators) ± Q_storage (PCM).

Examples: ISS uses an internal water loop and external ammonia radiators; Apollo used water-cooled garments with sublimators.
